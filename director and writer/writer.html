Writer

J.R.R. Tolkien, the renowned author of "The Lord of the Rings," is celebrated for crafting the enchanting realm of Middle-earth, where his epic trilogy unfolds. Prior to this monumental work, he penned "The Hobbit," chronicling the adventures of Bilbo Baggins, a hobbit who stumbles upon a magical ring. Tolkien's literary legacy extends beyond his compelling tales, as he was a philologist and a scholar, weaving his expertise into the creation of fictional languages like Elvish. His influence on the fantasy genre has been profound, inspiring countless writers, filmmakers, and gamers, and solidifying his position as a luminary in the world of fantasy literature.









Director

Peter Jackson, in full Sir Peter Robert Jackson, (born October 31, 1961, Pukerua Bay, North Island, New Zealand). Jackson won the rights to film Tolkien's epic in 1997 after meeting with producer Saul Zaentz. Originally working with Miramax Films towards a two-film production, Jackson was later pressured to render the story as a single film, and finally overcame a tight deadline by making a last-minute deal with New Line, who were keen on a trilogy.
